## [1] Overview

I recommend reviewing the following concepts, they will probably come up in recitation:

- ^^The first law of thermodynamics^^
    - *$\Delta U = Q - W$*
    - *taking $W$ to mean work done by the gas*
    - *keeping track of signs carefully*

- ^^Internal energy of a monatomic ideal gas^^
    - *$U = \tfrac32 nRT$*
    - *so $\Delta U = \tfrac32 nR\Delta T$*
    - *internal energy depends only on temperature*

- ^^Special thermodynamic processes^^
    - *isothermal: $\Delta T = 0$, so $\Delta U = 0$*
    - *constant volume: $W = 0$*
    - *adiabatic: $Q = 0$*

- ^^Work from a $PV$ diagram^^
    - *area under the curve*
    - *positive for expansion*
    - *negative for compression*

- ^^Using $PV=nRT$ to compare states on a diagram^^
    - *if $n$ is fixed, then $T \propto PV$*
    - *using endpoint states to find temperature changes*

- ^^Heat engines and thermal efficiency^^
    - *$e = \dfrac{W}{Q_H}$*
    - *$W = Q_H - Q_C$*
    - *understanding what is absorbed, exhausted, and converted to work*

- ^^Carnot efficiency^^
    - *$e_{\mathrm{Carnot}} = 1 - \dfrac{T_C}{T_H}$*
    - *reservoir temperatures must be in Kelvin*
    - *real engines must have efficiency below Carnot*

### [2.1] First Law, Internal Energy, and Special Processes

1. ★☆☆ <br> A rigid container holds $0.250 \ \mathrm{mol}$ of a monatomic ideal gas. The gas is heated from $290 \ \mathrm{K}$ to $360 \ \mathrm{K}$.
    - (a) Find the change in internal energy.
    - (b) Find the work done by the gas.
    - (c) Find the heat added to the gas.

    ??? success "Solution"
        Because the container is rigid, the volume is constant, so

        $$W = 0.$$

        For a monatomic ideal gas,

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 nR\Delta T.$$

        Here,

        $$\Delta T = 360 - 290 = 70 \ \mathrm{K}.$$

        So

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 (0.250)(8.31)(70)=2.18 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Using the first law,

        $$\Delta U = Q - W.$$

        Since $W=0$, we get

        $$Q = \Delta U = 2.18 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $\Delta U = +2.18 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}$; (b) $W=0$; (c) $Q = +2.18 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}$.

1. ★★☆ <br> A sample of $0.400 \ \mathrm{mol}$ of a monatomic ideal gas expands isothermally at $310 \ \mathrm{K}$ from $2.00 \ \mathrm{L}$ to $5.00 \ \mathrm{L}$.
    - (a) Find the change in internal energy.
    - (b) Find the work done by the gas.
    - (c) State whether heat flows into or out of the gas, and how much.

    ??? success "Solution"
        Since the process is isothermal for an ideal gas, the temperature does not change, so

        $$\Delta U = 0.$$

        The work done during an isothermal expansion is

        $$W = nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{V_f}{V_i}\right).$$

        So

        $$W = (0.400)(8.31)(310)\ln\!\left(\frac{5.00}{2.00}\right).$$

        This gives

        $$W = 9.44 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Using the first law,

        $$\Delta U = Q - W.$$

        Since $\Delta U = 0$, we get

        $$Q = W = 9.44 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Because $Q$ is positive, heat flows **into** the gas.

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $\Delta U = 0$; (b) $W = +9.44 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}$; (c) $Q = +9.44 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}$, so heat flows into the gas.

1. ★★★ <br> A monatomic ideal gas undergoes an adiabatic compression. During the compression, $2.60 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}$ of work is done **on** the gas. The gas contains $1.80 \ \mathrm{mol}$ and its initial temperature is $300 \ \mathrm{K}$.
    - (a) How much heat is exchanged with the surroundings?
    - (b) What is the change in internal energy of the gas?
    - (c) What is the final temperature of the gas?

    ??? success "Solution"
        Since the process is adiabatic,

        $$Q=0.$$

        The problem says work is done **on** the gas, so the work done **by** the gas is negative:

        $$W = -2.60 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Using the first law,

        $$\Delta U = Q - W = 0 - (-2.60 \times 10^3)=+2.60 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        For a monatomic ideal gas,

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 nR\Delta T.$$

        Therefore,

        $$\Delta T = \frac{\Delta U}{\tfrac32 nR} = \frac{2.60 \times 10^3}{\tfrac32(1.80)(8.31)}=1.16 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{K}.$$

        So the final temperature is

        $$T_f = 300 + 116 = 416 \ \mathrm{K}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $Q=0$; (b) $\Delta U = +2.60 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}$; (c) $T_f \approx 416 \ \mathrm{K}$.

### [2.2] $PV$ Diagrams, Work, and Heat

1. ★☆☆ <br> A gas expands at constant pressure $P = 220 \ \mathrm{kPa}$ from $1.50 \ \mathrm{L}$ to $3.80 \ \mathrm{L}$. What is the work done by the gas?

    ??? success "Solution"
        For a constant-pressure process,

        $$W = P\Delta V.$$

        Here,

        $$\Delta V = 3.80 - 1.50 = 2.30 \ \mathrm{L}.$$

        So

        $$W = (220 \ \mathrm{kPa})(2.30 \ \mathrm{L})=506 \ \mathrm{kPa \cdot L}.$$

        Using $1\,\mathrm{kPa\cdot L}=1\,\mathrm{J}$,

        $$W = 506 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** $W = +506 \ \mathrm{J}$.

1. ★★☆ <br> A monatomic ideal gas goes from state $A$ to state $C$ in two steps on a $PV$ diagram:
    - $A \to B$: constant volume from $(2.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ 150 \ \mathrm{kPa})$ to $(2.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ 300 \ \mathrm{kPa})$
    - $B \to C$: constant pressure from $(2.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ 300 \ \mathrm{kPa})$ to $(5.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ 300 \ \mathrm{kPa})$

    If the temperature at point $A$ is $240 \ \mathrm{K}$, find:
    - (a) the temperature at point $C$
    - (b) the total work done by the gas
    - (c) the change in internal energy
    - (d) the total heat added to the gas

    ??? success "Solution"
        Since $n$ is fixed for an ideal gas,

        $$T \propto PV.$$

        Therefore,

        $$\frac{T_C}{T_A} = \frac{P_CV_C}{P_AV_A} = \frac{(300)(5.00)}{(150)(2.00)} = 5.00.$$

        So

        $$T_C = 5.00(240) = 1200 \ \mathrm{K}.$$

        For the work:

        - Along $A \to B$, the volume is constant, so $W_{AB}=0$.
        - Along $B \to C$, the pressure is constant, so

        $$W_{BC}=P\Delta V=(300)(5.00-2.00)=900 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Thus,

        $$W_{\text{tot}} = 900 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        For a monatomic ideal gas,

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 nR\Delta T.$$

        But since $nRT=PV$, we can write

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 (P_CV_C - P_AV_A).$$

        So

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 [(300)(5.00) - (150)(2.00)]$$

        $$\Delta U = \frac32 (1500 - 300) = 1800 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Finally, from the first law,

        $$Q = \Delta U + W = 1800 + 900 = 2700 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $T_C = 1200 \ \mathrm{K}$; (b) $W = +900 \ \mathrm{J}$; (c) $\Delta U = +1800 \ \mathrm{J}$; (d) $Q = +2700 \ \mathrm{J}$.

1. ★★★ <br> A monatomic ideal gas is taken from state $A$ to state $C$, where

    $$A: (V=3.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ P=100 \ \mathrm{kPa},\ T=300 \ \mathrm{K})$$

    $$C: (V=5.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ P=300 \ \mathrm{kPa}).$$

    Compare two different paths between the same endpoints:

    - **Path 1:** a straight-line path on the $PV$ diagram from $A$ directly to $C$
    - **Path 2:** first go vertically upward at constant volume to $(3.00 \ \mathrm{L},\ 300 \ \mathrm{kPa})$, then horizontally right at constant pressure to $C$

    For each path, find:

    - (a) the work done by the gas
    - (b) the heat added to the gas

    Also find:

    - (c) the change in internal energy between $A$ and $C$
    - (d) which path requires more heat input

    ??? success "Solution"
        First find the change in internal energy. Since the endpoints are the same, $\Delta U$ is the same for both paths.

        For a monatomic ideal gas,

        $$\Delta U = \frac32(P_CV_C - P_AV_A).$$

        So

        $$\Delta U = \frac32[(300)(5.00) - (100)(3.00)]$$

        $$\Delta U = \frac32(1500 - 300)=1800 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        For **Path 1**, the pressure changes linearly, so the area under the curve is the area of a trapezoid:

        $$W_1 = \frac{P_A + P_C}{2}(V_C - V_A).$$

        Thus,

        $$W_1 = \frac{100 + 300}{2}(5.00 - 3.00) = 200(2.00)=400 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Then

        $$Q_1 = \Delta U + W_1 = 1800 + 400 = 2200 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        For **Path 2**:

        - The constant-volume part does no work.
        - The constant-pressure part gives

        $$W_2 = (300)(5.00-3.00)=600 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        So

        $$Q_2 = \Delta U + W_2 = 1800 + 600 = 2400 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        Comparing the two,

        $$Q_2 > Q_1.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $W_1 = 400 \ \mathrm{J}$ and $W_2 = 600 \ \mathrm{J}$; (b) $Q_1 = 2200 \ \mathrm{J}$ and $Q_2 = 2400 \ \mathrm{J}$; (c) $\Delta U = +1800 \ \mathrm{J}$ for both paths; (d) Path 2 requires more heat input.

### [2.3] Heat Engines and Carnot Efficiency

1. ★☆☆ <br> A heat engine absorbs $2.40 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}$ of heat each cycle and operates with efficiency $28.0\%$.
    - (a) How much work does it do per cycle?
    - (b) How much heat does it exhaust per cycle?

    ??? success "Solution"
        The efficiency is

        $$e = \frac{W}{Q_H}.$$

        So the work done per cycle is

        $$W = eQ_H = (0.280)(2.40 \times 10^3)=6.72 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        The exhaust heat is

        $$Q_C = Q_H - W = 2.40 \times 10^3 - 6.72 \times 10^2 = 1.73 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $W = 6.72 \times 10^2 \ \mathrm{J}$; (b) $Q_C = 1.73 \times 10^3 \ \mathrm{J}$.

1. ★★☆ <br> An engine absorbs heat at a rate of $18.0 \ \mathrm{kW}$ and exhausts heat at a rate of $11.5 \ \mathrm{kW}$.
    - (a) What is its thermal efficiency?
    - (b) How much work does it do in $3.00 \ \mathrm{min}$?

    ??? success "Solution"
        The power output is the difference between the input and exhaust heat rates:

        $$P = \dot{Q}_H - \dot{Q}_C = 18.0 - 11.5 = 6.5 \ \mathrm{kW}.$$

        The efficiency is

        $$e = \frac{P}{\dot{Q}_H} = \frac{6.5}{18.0}=0.361.$$

        So

        $$e = 36.1\%.$$

        Over a time of

        $$t = 3.00 \ \mathrm{min} = 180 \ \mathrm{s},$$

        the work done is

        $$W = Pt = (6.5 \times 10^3)(180)=1.17 \times 10^6 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $36.1\%$; (b) $1.17 \times 10^6 \ \mathrm{J}$.

1. ★★★ <br> A heat engine operates at $65.0\%$ of the efficiency of a Carnot engine working between a hot reservoir at $500^\circ\mathrm{C}$ and a cold reservoir at $40.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$. If the engine absorbs $3.20 \times 10^4 \ \mathrm{J}$ of heat each cycle, find:
    - (a) the actual efficiency of the engine
    - (b) the work done per cycle
    - (c) the heat exhausted per cycle

    ??? success "Solution"
        First convert the temperatures to Kelvin:

        $$T_H = 500 + 273 = 773 \ \mathrm{K}, \qquad T_C = 40.0 + 273 = 313 \ \mathrm{K}.$$

        The Carnot efficiency is

        $$e_{\mathrm{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} = 1 - \frac{313}{773}=0.595.$$

        The actual engine operates at $65.0\%$ of this value, so

        $$e = 0.650(0.595)=0.387.$$

        Thus the actual efficiency is about

        $$e = 38.7\%.$$

        The work done per cycle is

        $$W = eQ_H = (0.387)(3.20 \times 10^4)=1.24 \times 10^4 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        The heat exhausted is

        $$Q_C = Q_H - W = 3.20 \times 10^4 - 1.24 \times 10^4 = 1.96 \times 10^4 \ \mathrm{J}.$$

        <br>**Answer:** (a) $38.7\%$; (b) $W \approx 1.24 \times 10^4 \ \mathrm{J}$; (c) $Q_C \approx 1.96 \times 10^4 \ \mathrm{J}$.
